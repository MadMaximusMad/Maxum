# Capital & Revenue

## Revenue Sources

Maxum generates revenue from activity occurring within the system.

This includes trading fees produced by market interactions, as well as fees generated by applications that rely on Maxum’s liquidity layer. In addition, value is captured through providing liquidity as a service across markets.

Because these sources are tied to usage, revenue reflects real economic behavior rather than artificial incentives. As activity increases, revenue grows, reinforcing the system’s ability to deploy capital.

## Fee Mechanism (3%)

Each transaction involving MAX contributes to the system through a fixed fee mechanism.

When a transaction occurs, a portion of its value is captured as a fee and distributed across three functions: supply reduction, participant rewards, and capital growth. This ensures that every interaction contributes to the system.

For example, if one million dollars in trading volume occurs, thirty thousand dollars is collected in fees. Half of that amount is removed from circulation through burn, a portion is distributed to liquidity providers, and the remainder is added to the capital base.

This structure ensures that activity strengthens the system rather than extracting value from it.

## Capital Base

The capital base represents the total assets controlled by the system and serves as its economic foundation.

It includes stable assets, accumulated revenue, and liquidity positions deployed across markets. This capital is used to support liquidity, maintain stability, and enable expansion.

As the capital base grows, the system gains increased capacity to support markets and sustain activity. It provides the underlying strength that allows Maxum to operate as a continuous system.

## Capital Deployment

Capital within Maxum is continuously deployed rather than held passively.

It is used to create and deepen liquidity across markets, ensuring that trading conditions remain efficient. Revenue generated by the system is reinvested, allowing the capital base to expand and increasing the system’s ability to support additional markets.

This creates a compounding dynamic in which growth in activity leads to growth in liquidity, which in turn enables further activity.
